# Pivot Scenarios: If This Fails, What Else Could We Build?

**Date:** 2026-03-17
**Purpose:** Prepare 3 alternative directions before we need them
**Mental Model:** "If the door closes, which window opens?"

---

## Why Plan Pivots Now?

**The reality:**
- 90% of startups pivot
- Best pivots happen EARLY, not late
- Having backup plans reduces emotional attachment
- We might discover the pivot is better than the original idea

**The anti-pattern:**
- Fall in love with solution
- Ignore signals to pivot
- Run out of money before exploring alternatives
- End up with nothing

**Better approach:**
- Map out pivots now, while we have time
- Test pivot hypotheses in parallel
- Be ready to switch quickly if needed

## Pivot #1: Narrow the Market (Same Problem, Different Customer)

### The Pivot
Instead of targeting ALL 3-20 agent teams, focus on a specific segment:

**Option A: Luxury Real Estate Teams**
- Higher commissions ($50K+ per deal)
- More complex transactions (need coordination MORE)
- Willing to pay more ($299-499/month)
- Smaller market but more profitable

**Option B: Commercial Real Estate**
- Even more complex transactions
- Less competition (no one's building for commercial)
- Higher stakes = higher willingness to pay
- Different features needed

**Option C: New Agent Onboarding**
- Target NEW agents (first 2 years)
- They don't have systems yet
- They're desperate for help
- Partner with coaching programs

### Why This Might Work Better
- Less competition in niches
- Higher willingness to pay in some segments
- Can build deeper features for specific use cases
- Easier to market to specific communities

### Why It Might Fail
- Markets might be too small
- Same trust/accuracy problems
- Still competing with incumbents eventually

### How to Test
- Talk to 5 luxury agents: "Would you pay $299 for this?"
- Talk to 5 commercial agents: "Do you have this problem?"
- Talk to 5 new agents: "What's your biggest challenge?"

**Timeline to validate:** 2 weeks

## Pivot #2: Change the Solution (Same Customer, Different Problem)

### The Pivot
Keep targeting 3-20 agent teams, but solve a DIFFERENT problem:

**Option A: AI for Lead Generation**
- Extract data from lead inquiries
- Auto-respond and qualify leads
- Integrate with CRM
- Why: Lead gen is #1 priority for most agents

**Option B: AI for Comparative Market Analysis (CMA)**
- Automated property valuations
- Pull comps, analyze market data
- Generate reports for clients
- Why: Every agent does CMAs, currently manual

**Option C: AI for Client Communication**
- Automated check-ins with clients
- Transaction status updates
- Personalized messaging
- Why: Agents hate admin communication, clients love updates

**Option D: AI for Marketing Content**
- Generate listing descriptions (already exists)
- Social media content
- Email campaigns
- Why: 82% already use AI for this, but maybe we do it better

### Why This Might Work Better
- Lower stakes than contract data (less accuracy needed)
- More visible results (agents see the output)
- Faster time-to-value (immediate vs. waiting for deadlines)
- Different competitive landscape

### Why It Might Fail
- More competition (lead gen is crowded)
- Might not be painful enough
- Existing solutions might be good enough

### How to Test
- Ask agents: "What takes the most time in your day?"
- Ask agents: "What would you pay to automate?"
- Look for tasks that are: time-consuming, repeatable, low-stakes

**Timeline to validate:** 2 weeks

## Pivot #3: Platform Play (Build for a Different Industry)

### The Pivot
Apply the same AI extraction/coordination concept to a different industry:

**Option A: Legal Industry (Small Law Firms)**
- Contract review and deadline management
- Case management automation
- Compliance tracking
- Why: Lawyers have similar problems, higher willingness to pay

**Option B: Healthcare (Small Practices)**
- Patient document processing
- Insurance claim tracking
- Appointment scheduling
- Why: Admin burden is huge, regulations create deadlines

**Option C: Construction/Contractors**
- Project milestone tracking
- Permit deadline management
- Subcontractor coordination
- Why: Complex projects, many deadlines, currently manual

**Option D: Insurance (Claims Processing)**
- Document extraction from claims
- Deadline management for claims
- Compliance tracking
- Why: High volume, repetitive, error-prone

### Why This Might Work Better
- Some industries have HIGHER willingness to pay
- Less competition (everyone's focused on real estate)
- Better unit economics in some verticals
- Regulatory complexity creates urgency

### Why It Might Fail
- We don't have domain expertise
- Sales cycles might be longer
- Regulatory barriers to entry
- Might be harder to reach customers

### How to Test
- Talk to 5 lawyers: "Do you have deadline management problems?"
- Talk to 5 contractors: "How do you track project milestones?"
- Look for industries where: deadlines matter, documents are complex, stakes are high

**Timeline to validate:** 3 weeks (need to learn new industries)

## Pivot #4: B2B2C (Sell to Brokerages, Not Agents)

### The Pivot
Instead of selling to individual teams, sell to brokerages who then give it to agents:

**The Pitch:**
- Brokerages provide tools to agents
- We sell to the brokerage (1 sale = 50-500 agents)
- Brokerage gets compliance + agent productivity
- We get distribution

### Why This Might Work Better
- Higher LTV (enterprise contracts)
- Lower CAC (one sale vs. 100 individual sales)
- Built-in distribution
- Brokerage has budget, individual agents don't

### Why It Might Fail
- Longer sales cycles (6-12 months)
- Need enterprise features (SSO, compliance, etc.)
- Brokerages are slow to adopt new tech
- Already locked into existing vendors

### How to Test
- Talk to 5 brokerage owners: "Do you provide tools to agents?"
- Talk to 5 brokerage tech buyers: "What's your budget for agent tools?"
- Look for mid-sized brokerages (50-200 agents) - sweet spot

**Timeline to validate:** 4 weeks

## Pivot #5: Feature, Not Product (Get Acquired)

### The Pivot
Instead of building a standalone business, build a feature to be acquired:

**Potential Acquirers:**
- Dotloop/Skyslope (add AI to existing product)
- CRM companies (Follow Up Boss, Salesforce)
- Real estate coaching companies (need tools for clients)
- PropTech companies expanding into AI

**The Strategy:**
- Build best-in-class AI extraction
- Get 1,000-5,000 users
- Demonstrate clear value
- Shop ourselves to acquirers
- Exit for $2-10M (acquihire + technology)

### Why This Might Work Better
- Lower bar for success
- Can exit in 18-24 months
- Don't need to build a real business
- Let someone else handle distribution

### Why It Might Fail
- Acquirers might just build it themselves
- Might not get enough users to be interesting
- Acquisition market might be weak
- Might not get the valuation we want

### How to Test
- Talk to 3 potential acquirers: "Would you be interested in this technology?"
- Get a sense of acquisition prices for similar companies
- Look at recent PropTech acquisitions

**Timeline to validate:** 2 weeks (informal conversations)

## The "Don't Bother" Pivots

These seem obvious but are bad ideas:

### ❌ Pivot to Consumer
- Selling to homebuyers directly
- Problem: No money, no urgency, one-time use

### ❌ Pivot to Consulting
- Become a transaction coordination service
- Problem: Not scalable, low margins

### ❌ Pivot to Marketplace
- Connect agents with TCs
- Problem: Chicken-egg problem, low take rate

### ❌ Pivot to Cryptocurrency/Web3
- Tokenize real estate transactions
- Problem: Solution looking for a problem

## The Pre-Pivot Checklist

Before pivoting, answer these questions:

**About the Original Idea:**
- [ ] Did we talk to 50+ potential customers?
- [ ] Did we build an MVP and get real usage?
- [ ] Did we try 3+ acquisition channels?
- [ ] Did we iterate based on feedback?
- [ ] Are we quitting because it's hard, or because it's wrong?

**About the Pivot:**
- [ ] Is the problem validated (not just hypothesized)?
- [ ] Is the market big enough?
- [ ] Is the competition manageable?
- [ ] Do we have some advantage?
- [ ] Can we reach customers profitably?
- [ ] Are we excited about it, or just desperate?

**If we can't check most boxes:** Don't pivot yet, keep testing original idea.

## The Emotional Side of Pivoting

**The hard truth:**
- Pivoting feels like failure (it's not)
- Sunk cost fallacy will make us want to keep going
- Ego will prevent us from admitting we're wrong
- We'll want "just one more month"

**How to combat this:**
- Set clear kill criteria (see kill-criteria.md)
- Plan pivots now, when we're rational
- Get external advisors to tell us the truth
- Celebrate pivots as learning, not failure

**The goal:** Build something people want, even if it's not what we started with.

**This is dangerous.**

The best time to explore pivots is when we DON'T need them yet.
The worst time is when we're desperate.

**Action:** In the next 2 weeks, have informal conversations about 2-3 pivot options.
Not to pursue them, but to know they exist.
